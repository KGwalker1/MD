## 1. HISTORICAL CONTEXT AND BACKGROUND

### The Post-Independence Expansion (1947-1970s)

**Massive Growth**:

- From 3 psychology departments at independence (1947) to **32 departments by end of 1960s**
- UGC (established 1956) provided generous funding for expansion
- ICSSR supported research projects
- International exchange programs (Commonwealth, Fulbright, Ford Foundation) sent scholars to UK, US, Canada for training

**The Problem**:

- Psychology expanded quantitatively but **not qualitatively**
- Research remained imitative of Western models
- "Psychology in India was dominated by scholars trained abroad for the next two decades"
- Focus on "safe" laboratory topics: perception, memory, learning, reaction time

**The Disconnect**:

- Indian psychologists were studying Western problems with Western methods
- Little attention to India's unique social realities: poverty, caste, communalism, illiteracy, population explosion
- "The poverty of social psychology is reflected in its set definitions"

## 2. THE CRISIS OF RELEVANCE (MID-1970s)

### The Turning Point

**Growing Disillusionment**:
By the mid-1970s, Indian psychologists began realizing that:

- **60+ years of Western psychology in India had not yielded significant discoveries**
- Research was "dull and replicative"
- Psychology was **socially irrelevant** - not addressing India's pressing problems
- Psychologists were becoming **marginalized in national development** discourse

**The "Left Out" Feeling** :

> "The feeling of 'left out' has sprouted among psychologists a strong urge to make their research efforts relevant to the process of national development"

### The Core Problem: Two Worlds Phenomenon

Indian psychologists lived in **two different worlds**:

- **Professional world**: Western scientific psychology, value-free research, laboratory experiments
- **Personal world**: Indian cultural values, traditional wisdom, concern for social problems

**Manifestations**:

- Psychologists shared socio-cultural context with Indian masses but didn't integrate it professionally
- "Indian psychology has become not only imitative and subservient but also dull and replicative"
- "Replications and imitations have sometimes taken us to ridiculous lengths that we have studied caste like a race, communalism like capitalism and untouchables as American blacks"

## 3. KEY CRITICS AND THEIR CONTRIBUTIONS

### A. Ashis Nandy (1974)

**The Seminal Critique**:

> "Indian psychology has become not merely imitative and subservient but also dull and replicative"

**Key Arguments**:

- Psychology of dissent needed - opposition to banality of caste, gender, and class politics
- Indian psychologists were "recipients rather than exchange agents of knowledge"
- Flow of knowledge was one-directional (West to India)
- Research patterned on American studies - a country without India's problems

**Nandy's Diagnosis**:

- "The non-paradigmatic crisis in Indian Psychology"
- Struggle was about "sticking to the paradigm of being a good scientist" rather than addressing real issues
- Psychology was more attuned to "processes of doing science rather than falling into the reflexivity of criticality"

### B. K.G. Agrawal (1973)

**The Term "Adoptology"**:

- Coined to describe Indian psychology as merely adopting Western theories without critical examination
- No original contribution or adaptation to Indian context

### C. Durganand Sinha (1977)

**The Call for Indigenous Psychology**:

> "The goal of indigenization is the development of 'appropriate psychology' and not 'parochial psychology'"

**Key Contributions**:

- Called for psychology with **own paradigms** rooted in Indian soil
- Distinguished between **exogenous indigenization** (adapting Western psychology) and **endogenous indigenization** (developing from Indian traditions)
- Emphasized that Western models must adapt to India's cultural realities
- "One of the architects of modern Indian psychology"

**Sinha's Vision** :

- Psychology must address "problems vital to the country"
- Need for "assimilative synthesis" of indigenous and modern knowledge
- Psychology should be "culturally anchored" with "unique character that fits with the country's socio-cultural ethos"

### D. J.B.P. Sinha (1973)

**Leadership in Social Psychology**:

- Urged scientific understanding of Indian social reality through indigenous knowledge
- Later developed "Nurturant Task Leadership" (1980) - culture-specific organizational theory
- Contributed to organizational psychology relevant to Indian context

### E. Other Critics

- **Mukherjee (1980)**: Emphasized relevance to social reality
- **Pareek (1981)**: Called for commitment to social values and national needs

## 4. MAJOR SOCIAL ISSUES ADDRESSED

### The Shift in Research Focus

**From**: Laboratory studies of perception, memory, learning
**To**: Social problems affecting millions of Indians

### A. Communalism and Communal Violence

**Historical Context**:

- Partition of India (1947) led to massive Hindu-Muslim riots
- Thousands died; millions displaced
- Need to understand causes and solutions

**Major Research**:

- **Gardner Murphy's UNESCO Project (1949-1953)**
  - Collaboration between Indian and Western psychologists
  - Culminated in book: "In the Minds of Men" (1953)
  - Studied causes of communal tensions

**Later Studies**:

- Attitudes toward communalism (Adinarayan, 1953, 1958)
- Intergroup relations and violence (De Ridder and Tripathi, 1992)
- Social identity processes (Pandey et al., 2014)

### B. Caste and Social Stratification

**Research Focus**:

- Caste attitudes and prejudice
- Inter-caste relations
- Reservation policy effects
- Dalit identity and psychology

**Key Studies**:

- **Adinarayan (1953, 1958)**: Measured communal and caste attitudes
- **Rath & Sircar (1960)**: Inter-caste attitudes
- **Anant (1970)**: Inter-caste attitudes and behavior
- **Paranjape (1970)**: "Caste, Prejudice and Individual"
- **Jogdand (2015)**: Caste-based humiliation

**Theoretical Development**:

- Understanding caste as psychological phenomenon
- Internalized oppression
- Identity formation among marginalized groups

### C. Poverty and Economic Disadvantage

**The Issue**:

- Massive poverty in post-independence India
- Need for psychological understanding of deprivation

**Research Areas**:

- **Psychology of poverty**: Effects on cognition, motivation, mental health
- **Rural psychology**: Farmers, agricultural laborers, landless workers
- **Tribal psychology**: Indigenous communities, displacement, modernization
- **Deprivation and disadvantage**: Effects on development

**Durganand Sinha's Work** :

- Studied rural farmers, tribal people, impoverished and disadvantaged
- "Psychology in a Third World Country: The Indian Experience" (1986)
- Concept of "extension motivation" - motivation beyond self-interest
- "Dependency proneness" in Indian context
- "Dissatisfaction-based achievement motivation"

### D. National Integration and Social Change

**Focus Areas**:

- **Education for national integration**: Reducing communal and caste divisions through education
- **Social change psychology**: Rapid transformation and its psychological effects
- **Youth unrest and agitations**: Student movements, political participation
- **Urbanization**: Migration, slums, identity issues

**Durganand Sinha's Contribution** :

> "Changes that formerly took centuries to materialise in the West are now happening in the span of a generation or two... This kind of transformation is discordant and chaotic"

- Studied students, industrial workers, rural communities
- Resourceful in using multiple approaches to fit people's needs and culture

### E. Population and Family Planning

**The Crisis**:

- Rapid population growth threatening development
- Need for psychology of family planning behavior

**Research Areas**:

- Attitudes toward family planning
- Motivation for small family norm
- Communication strategies
- Gender roles and reproductive health

### F. Other Social Issues

## 5. SPECIFIC RESEARCH AREAS AND STUDIES

### A. Social Psychology of Attitudes and Prejudice

**Systematic Surveys** (1960s-1970s):

- Attitudes on caste, religion, and development
- Prejudice measurement and reduction
- Intergroup relations

**Methodological Innovation**:

- Adaptation of Western scales to Indian context
- Development of indigenous measures
- Field studies rather than laboratory experiments

### B. Industrial and Organizational Psychology

**Shift from**:

- Job attitudes, work incentives, absenteeism (1950s-60s)

**To**:

- **Nurturant Task Leadership** (J.B.P. Sinha, 1980)
  - Combines Indian cultural elements (nurturance, dependency) with Western management
  - Contingency approach with personalized relationship
- **Rural industrialization**: Small-scale industries, cooperatives
- **Labor relations**: Unions, worker participation

### C. Cross-Cultural Psychology

**International Collaboration**:

- Indian psychologists joined global cross-cultural projects (1970s-90s)
- Testing Western theories across cultures
- Adaptation of measurement instruments (PGI Memory Scale, intelligence tests)

**Critical Reflection**:

- Realized that simply applying Western models to Indian samples was insufficient
- Need for culture-specific theories
- Led to **indigenization movement**

### D. Community and Rural Psychology

**Focus**:

- Village-level studies
- Community mental health
- Participatory action research
- Grassroots interventions

**Examples**:

- Studies on rural farmers
- Tribal communities and modernization
- Peasant movements and social change

## 6. INSTITUTIONAL AND METHODOLOGICAL CHANGES

### A. Research Outside Universities

**The Problem**:

- Difficult to conduct research in bureaucratic university setup
- Campus unrest, heavy teaching loads, no research incentives

**The Shift**:

- Psychologists moved to **research institutes**
- More conducive environment for social research

**Major Research Institutes** :
| Institute | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| A.N.S. Institute of Social Studies, Patna | Social research |
| Centre for Study of Developing Societies, Delhi | Development studies |
| National Institute of Community Development, Hyderabad | Community development |
| NCERT | Educational research |
| NIEPA | Educational policy |
| NIPCCD | Child development |
| NIHFW | Health and family welfare |
| Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore | Applied research |
| Indian Statistical Institute, Calcutta | Statistical research |

### B. Methodological Innovations

**From Laboratory to Field**:

- **Field studies**: Real-world settings rather than laboratories
- **Survey research**: Large-scale attitude and opinion surveys
- **Participatory methods**: Involving communities in research
- **Action research**: Research leading to social intervention

**Interdisciplinary Approaches** :

- Collaboration with sociology, economics, political science, anthropology
- Drawing insights from multiple disciplines
- Greater emphasis on fieldwork and qualitative methods

### C. ICSSR and Funding for Social Research

**Indian Council of Social Science Research (ICSSR)**:

- Major funding for social science research including psychology
- Supported problem-oriented research
- Three sets of surveys of research in psychology (sponsored by ICSSR)
  - Showed imitative nature of research
  - Stimulated innovative studies from sociocultural milieu

## 7. THE INDIGENIZATION MOVEMENT EMERGES

### The 1970s as Turning Point

**Four Factors Behind Indigenization** :

1. **Disenchantment with Western psychology**
   - Inconsistent findings and unconfirmed theories
   - Compelled psychologists to think afresh

2. **Surging nationalism**
   - Trend toward de-colonization of psychological knowledge
   - Searching philosophical roots of Indian wisdom
   - Retrieving indigenous concepts from ancient texts

3. **Confronted by problems of national development**
   - Communal conflicts, poverty, inequality
   - Western psychology did not offer usable solutions
   - Even appropriate perspectives lacking

4. **Formation of IACCP (International Association of Cross-Cultural Psychology)**
   - Rallying ground for non-Western psychologists
   - Collaboration with Western psychologists (John Berry, Harry Triandis)
   - Facilitated alternative thinking

### Definition of Indigenization

**Durganand Sinha (1977, 1986, 1993)**:

> "The process of making the discipline appropriate/suited to the socio-cultural soil of the country"

**Kim (1990)** :
Indigenous psychology is:

- (a) Native
- (b) Not transported from another region
- (c) Designed for its people
- (d) About understanding how local context affects psychological description, explanation, and application

**Key Elements** :

- Primacy of indigenous/local/culturally derived perspective
- Relevance to indigenous culture/people
- Indigenous culture as source of concepts and theories
- Rather than imposed theories from outside

### Two Types of Indigenization

| Type                          | Description                                          | Approach                                                               |
| ----------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Exogenous Indigenization**  | Integration of modern psychology with Indian thought | Start with Western tests/measures, adapt to Indian conditions          |
| **Endogenous Indigenization** | Development from Indian philosophical perspective    | Start with Indian texts and traditions, develop psychological concepts |

## 9. IMPACT AND LEGACY

### Immediate Impact (1970s-1980s)

**Positive Changes**:

- Psychology became more **socially relevant**
- Research began addressing **real problems** of Indian society
- **Indigenization movement** gained momentum
- **Interdisciplinary collaboration** increased
- **Field research** became more common

**Limitations**:

- Still largely **conformist** to Western paradigms
- "Apolitical, neutral and rudimentary" political strategy
- Failed to fully integrate **critical perspectives**
- "Lack of effort to raise critical issues, engage in movements"

### Long-term Legacy (1980s-Present)

**The 1980s**:

- Indigenization picked up momentum
- Integration of indigenous and foreign ideas, concepts, methods

**The 1990s**:

- Paradigmatic concerns
- Disciplinary identity crisis
- "What is Indian psychology?"

**2000s-Present**:

- **Indian Psychology** emerges in academia
- Integration of traditional (Yoga, Vedanta, Buddhism) with modern psychology
- "Psychology for India" movement

**Current Challenges** :

- Even in 21st century, priority remains on "Yoga, Vedanta, spirituality and consciousness rooted in Indian ancient scriptures"
- Psychology still on "backbench" in national planning compared to other social sciences
- Need for "problem-oriented research in interdisciplinary and multidisciplinary fashion"
