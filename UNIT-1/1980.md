## 1. HISTORICAL CONTEXT: BUILDING ON THE 1970s

### The Foundation

The 1980s indigenization movement was a **direct continuation and intensification** of the 1970s crisis:

**1970s Developments**:

- Growing disillusionment with Western psychology (Nandy's critique: "dull and replicative")
- Call for indigenous psychology with own paradigms (D. Sinha, 1977)
- Move to addressing social issues (poverty, caste, communalism)
- Recognition that Western theories didn't fit Indian reality

**The Transition**:

> "The signs of efforts to outgrow the alien frame were noticed in the 1970s. The search for a new identity became a major question. Culture-specific concepts, and a search for culturally appropriate methods and tools were emphasized and the relevance of culture was realized."

**1980s Intensification**:

- Indigenization picked up **momentum** in the 1980s
- Integration of indigenous and foreign ideas, concepts, methods
- "Blending scientific ways with indigenous concepts emerged as an important academic agenda"

## 2. DEFINITION AND MEANING OF INDIGENIZATION

### Durganand Sinha's Definition

> **"Indigenisation is the process of making the discipline sensitive to cultural nuances and social reality."**

> **"The goal of indigenization is the development of 'appropriate psychology' and not 'parochial psychology'."**

**Key Elements**:

- Making psychology **culturally sensitive**
- Developing **appropriate psychology** for India
- Not rejecting Western psychology entirely, but **adapting** it
- Creating psychology that fits Indian **socio-cultural ethos**

### Kim's Definition (1990)

Indigenous psychology is:

1. **Native** - originating from within the culture
2. **Not transported** from another region
3. **Designed for its people** - relevant to local population
4. **Context-sensitive** - understanding how local context affects psychological description, explanation, and application

### Core Characteristics :

| Feature                               | Description                                         |
| ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primacy of indigenous perspective** | Local/culturally derived viewpoint takes precedence |
| **Relevance to indigenous culture**   | Must be meaningful to local people                  |
| **Indigenous culture as source**      | Concepts and theories derived from local traditions |
| **Not imposed from outside**          | Rejection of wholesale import of foreign theories   |

## 3. KEY FEATURES OF THE 1980s INDIGENIZATION MOVEMENT

### A. Culture-Specific Concepts and Theories

**Development of Indian Concepts**:

- Moving beyond Western categories
- Creating theories rooted in Indian cultural context
- Testing Western theories for cultural fit

**Examples**:

- **"Nurturant Task Leadership"** (J.B.P. Sinha, 1980) - combining Indian nurturance with Western management
- **"Extension motivation"** (Pareek, 1968) - motivation beyond self-interest
- **"Dependency proneness"** (Sinha, 1968) - Indian social interdependence
- **"Achievement value"** (Mukherjee, 1974)
- **"Dissatisfaction-based achievement motivation"** (Mehta, 1972)

### B. Search for Culturally Appropriate Methods

**Methodological Innovations**:

- Adaptation of Western tests and measures to Indian conditions
- Development of indigenous measurement tools
- Qualitative methods alongside quantitative
- Field-based research over laboratory experiments

**Examples**:

- PGI Memory Scale (adapted for Indian context)
- Intelligence tests in Indian languages
- Indigenous attitude scales

### C. Integration of Traditional and Modern

**The Synthesis Approach**:

> "Gradually, blending scientific ways with indigenous concepts emerged as an important academic agenda"

**Key Integrations**:

- **Yoga and meditation** in psychology research
- **Ayurvedic concepts** in health psychology
- **Indian philosophical concepts** (Sankhya-Yoga, Vedanta, Buddhism)
- **Sufi traditions** in understanding self and identity

### D. Context-Sensitivity

**Ramanujan's Contribution**:

- Emphasized **context sensitivity** as key feature of Indian way of thinking
- Indians are **holistic** - combining excellence in work, personalized relationships, abstract thinking, emotionality, rationality, and spirituality

**Sinha and Pandey (2007)** :

- Indians function with **diverse mindsets** in different contexts
- **Materialist mindset** in multinational organizations
- **Dependence-prone/collectivist mindset** in family-owned, bureaucratic, traditional organizations
- **Holistic mindset** in organizations valuing both performance and people

### E. Interdisciplinary Approach

**Collaboration Across Disciplines**:

- Psychology + Sociology + Anthropology + Philosophy
- Drawing from Indian philosophy and religion
- Integration of humanities and social sciences

## 4. TWO ROUTES OF INDIGENIZATION

### Durganand Sinha's Classification

| Route                         | Description                                                             | Approach                                                               | Example                                                            |
| ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Endogenous Indigenization** | Purely product of culture; taking native concepts and categories        | Start with Indian texts and traditions, develop psychological concepts | Sankhya-Yoga psychology, Bhakti psychology, concepts from Ayurveda |
| **Exogenous Indigenization**  | Product of interaction of cultural variables with concepts from outside | Start with Western tests/measures, adapt to Indian conditions          | Adapting IQ tests, personality scales for Indian context           |

### The Dominant Route in 1980s: Exogenous

**Why Exogenous was Preferred**:

- Majority of Indian psychologists were **trained in Western models**
- Sudden shift to endogenous theories not feasible
- Natural to continue using Western concepts with **increasing sensitivity to cultural context**

**Adair's Observation (1989)** :

> "Psychology in India is gradually getting indigenised" through exogenous route - local adaptation of imported discipline

### The Endogenous Stream

**Intensification in 1980s-1990s**:

- Efforts to rejuvenate psychology in ancient texts **intensified in the 1980s and 1990s**
- Notable publications by Paranjpe (1984, 1998), Chakraborty (1995), Saraswathi (1999), K. Ramakrishna Rao (2002, 2005)

**Key Publications**:

- Ramachandra Rao: _Development of Psychological Thought in India_ (1962)
- Ragunath Safaya: _Indian Psychology_ (1975)
- Kuppuswami: _Elements of Ancient Indian Psychology_ (1985)
- Paranjpe: _Self and Identity in Modern Psychology and Indian Thought_ (1984)

## 5. MAJOR AREAS OF INDIGENOUS DEVELOPMENT

### A. Social Cognition and Attribution

**Indigenous Developments**:

- **Attribution theory** with socially and culturally specific causal categories
- Tested for understanding **achievement, health, and other aspects** of human behavior
- Dalal (1988): Attribution theory adapted to Indian context

**Key Insight**: Western attribution patterns don't always apply; Indians have different causal explanations for success/failure

### B. Intelligence and Cognition

**Integral Intelligence** (Srivastava and Misra, 2007) :

- **Four dimensions**:
  1. Cognitive competence
  2. Social competence
  3. Competence in action
  4. Emotional competence
- Beyond Western IQ - holistic view of intelligence

**PASS Theory Extension** (Das et al., 2000) :

- Planning, Attention, Successive, and Simultaneous Processes
- Applied to various cognitive functions in Indian context

**Cognitive Processes in Eco-Cultural Settings** (R.C. Mishra, 1997) :

- How perception and memory get shaped under diverse eco-cultural settings
- Bio-ecological framework (Pirta, 2011) integrating ecology, biology, behavior in Himalayas

### C. Self and Identity

**Complexity of Indian Selfhood**:

- **Not simply individualist or collectivist**
- **"Coexistence of opposites"** (Sinha and Tripathi, 1994)
- Both individualist and collectivist aspects present

**Mascolo et al. (2004)** :

- **Four ways of conceptualizing selfhood**:
  1. Independent
  2. Interdependent
  3. Relational
  4. Encompassing

**Text-Based Analyses** :

- Paranjpe (1984, 1998): Indian views of self and identity
- Beg (1970): Concept of self in Sufi tradition
- **Higher/spiritual self** occupies important place in Indian traditions

### D. Organizational Psychology

**Nurturant Task Leadership** (J.B.P. Sinha, 1980) :

- **Culture-specific theory** for Indian organizations
- Combined:
  - Indian cultural elements: **nurturance, dependency, personalized relationship, status consciousness**
  - Western management: **contingency approach, reinforcement principles**

**Values in Management** (Chakraborty, 1995) :

- Critical role of values in managerial transformation
- Ethics in management from Indian perspective

**Beyond American and Japanese Models** (R. Gupta, 2002) :

- Need for models specific to Indian conditions
- Not imitating West or East, but developing Indian models

### E. Emotions and Cultural Psychology

**Recovery of Indigenous Concepts**:

- **Lajja** (shame) (Menon and Shweder, 1994)
- **Bhakti** (devotion to God) (Paranjpe, 1998)
- **Rasa** experience: Depersonalized, transcendental, transformative aspects (Paranjpe and Bhatt, 1997)

### F. Health and Well-Being

**Yoga and Meditation Research**:

- Neurophysiological studies of meditation
- Cross-cultural aspects of emotion and meditation
- Integration of Ayurvedic concepts

**Sankhya-Yoga Perspective** (Rao, 2002, 2011) :

- **Purusa** (consciousness) and **Prakrti** (matter)
- **Citta**: Functional mind comprising cognitive processes, ego, senses, instinctual tendencies (vasanas), effects of past actions (samskaras)
- Influence on cognitions and behavior

### G. Developmental Psychology

**Sinha's Pioneering Work** :

- First to emphasize **sociocultural context in cognitive development**
- Studied students, industrial workers, rural communities
- Resourceful in using multiple approaches fitting people's needs and culture

## 8. CRITICAL REFLECTIONS AND DEBATES

### A. Limitations of Cross-Cultural Approach

**Sinha's Critique (1997)** :

- Cross-cultural work led to **testing Western theories on Indian samples**
- Very rarely studies originated from needs of Indian society
- Very rarely tested Indian concepts in other cultures
- **Indiscriminate search for cultural differences** made ventures superficial
- Without providing much understanding of culture or contemporary problems

**Misra and Gergen (1993)** :

- To a large extent cross-cultural psychology remained a **methodological enterprise**
- **Culture remained a peripheral concern**

### B. The Endogenous vs. Exogenous Debate

**Tension**:

- Endogenous: Purely Indian, but may lack scientific rigor
- Exogenous: Scientifically rigorous, but may not be fully Indian

**Resolution Attempted**:

- "Blending scientific ways with indigenous concepts"
- Integration of both approaches

### C. Critique of Indigenization

**Nandy's Perspective**:

- Indigenization sometimes becomes **uncritical worship of the past**
- Need for criticality about Indian culture itself, not just Western psychology
- Sometimes claims of cultural superiority over Western models

**The Conformist vs. Critical Tension** :

- 1980s psychology remained largely **conformist** - sticking to scientific paradigm
- Failed to fully embrace **critical perspectives**
- "Apolitical, neutral and rudimentary" political strategy
- "Lack of effort to raise critical issues, engage in movements"

### D. The "Left Out" Problem

**Continued Marginalization**:

- Even in 1980s, psychology remained on **"backbench"** in national planning
- Other social sciences (economics, sociology, political science) more prominent
- Psychologists wanted to "break free from theoretical and methodological constraints"

## 9. IMPACT AND LEGACY

### Immediate Impact (1980s)

**Positive Changes**:

- Psychology became **more culturally sensitive**
- Development of **culture-specific concepts and theories**
- **Integration** of traditional Indian wisdom with modern psychology
- **Methodological innovations** appropriate to Indian context
- **Interdisciplinary collaboration** increased

**Publications Boom**:

- Research publications took "quantum jump"
- Many books on Indian psychology published
- Textbooks incorporating Indian perspectives

### Long-term Legacy (1990s-2000s)

**1990s: Paradigmatic Concerns**:

- Identity crisis: "What is Indian psychology?"
- Disciplinary identity questions
- Continued refinement of indigenous approaches

**2000s-Present: Indian Psychology in Academia** :

- **Handbook of Indian Psychology** (Rao, Paranjpe, Dalal, 2008)
- **Textbooks** on Indian psychology (Kuppuswami, 1985; Misra and Mohanty, 2002)
- Integration in teaching programs (Cornelissen, 2005)
- **Core curriculum**: Indian theories of self and consciousness

**Current Status** :

> "By the turn of this new millennium, the academic scenario is changing in India. The scientific community is better prepared now than in the past to accept a psychology rooted in native wisdom and philosophical texts."

**Challenges Remaining**:

- "Recipient nature of research endeavours, antiquated and obsolete teaching programmes, and lack of applied orientation have devoid the discipline of any professional momentum"
- Need to "grapple with the real issues of development and to act as social catalysts in the change process"
