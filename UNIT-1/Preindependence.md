## 1. INTRODUCTION AND HISTORICAL CONTEXT

### Definition

The **Pre-independence era** in Indian psychology refers to the period from the late 19th century to 1947, when modern academic psychology was introduced, established, and developed in India under British colonial rule .

### Historical Timeline Overview:

| Period               | Phase                | Characteristics                                                             |
| -------------------- | -------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Ancient-Medieval** | Indigenous Period    | Philosophical, spiritual psychology in texts                                |
| **1757-1857**        | Early Colonial       | British rule begins; Western education introduced                           |
| **1857-1900**        | Foundation Period    | Universities established; psychology part of philosophy                     |
| **1900-1916**        | Pre-institutional    | Syllabi drafted; laboratories planned                                       |
| **1916-1947**        | Institutional Period | Departments established; research begins; professional organizations formed |

### Colonial Context :

- Modern psychology arrived as part of British colonial education system
- No continuity with existing Indian psychological traditions
- Western psychology introduced as "ready-made intellectual package"
- Colonial government more interested in anthropology and sociology for governance than psychology
- Psychology primarily served teacher training needs

## 2. INDIGENOUS ROOTS: ANCIENT INDIAN PSYCHOLOGY

### Pre-Colonial Psychological Thought :

Before Western psychology arrived, India had sophisticated psychological concepts embedded in philosophical and spiritual traditions:

**Key Concepts:**

- **Karma**: Actions and their psychological consequences
- **Dharma**: Moral duty and psychological development
- **Moksha**: Spiritual liberation as psychological goal
- **Atma-vichara**: Inner exploration/self-inquiry
- **Atma-jnana**: Self-realization
- **Yoga**: Systematic practices for mind control
- **Meditation**: Techniques for consciousness development

**Sources:**

- **Patanjali's Yoga Sutras**: Systematic psychology of mind
- **Charaka Samhita**: Ayurvedic psychology
- **Upanishads**: Philosophy of consciousness
- **Bhagavad Gita**: Psychology of action and devotion
- **Buddhist texts**: Abhidhamma psychology

**Characteristics of Indigenous Psychology** :

- Integrated with spirituality and religion
- Holistic approach (mind-body-spirit unity)
- Practical and applied orientation
- Problem-solving focus
- Interdisciplinary nature
- Symbiosis between human and nature (not dichotomous as in West)

### Discontinuity :

> "During the British rule when modern psychology made a new beginning in India it did not have any continuity with the existing knowledge institutions and field of psychology. Western psychology with laboratory work was a novel approach, not having any parallel in traditional Indian psychology."

## 3. COLONIAL IMPACT AND WESTERN INFLUENCE

### British Educational Policy :

**Key Developments:**

- **1857**: First universities established (Calcutta, Madras, Bombay)
- Psychology initially taught as part of **Philosophy** courses
- Focus on **Experimental Psychology** following German (Wundtian) tradition
- **Teacher training** centers set up with psychology in curriculum
- Emphasis on **scientific, laboratory-based** research

### Western Theoretical Influences:

| School                              | Influence on Indian Psychology                                                |
| ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Wundt's Experimental Psychology** | Dominant in early Calcutta school; psychophysics, sensation, perception       |
| **Freudian Psychoanalysis**         | Strong influence through Girindrasekhar Bose; Indian Psychoanalytical Society |
| **Titchener's Structuralism**       | Laboratory research methodology                                               |
| **Spearman's Factor Theory**        | Mental testing tradition at Mysore                                            |
| **Allport's Social Psychology**     | Early social psychology research                                              |

### Psychiatric Developments :

- **1787**: First mental hospital at Kolkata established
- **1745**: Mental hospital in Bombay
- **1794**: Mental hospital at Kizhpakkam, Madras
- Early treatments: Morphine, opium, hot baths, leeches, music therapy, cannabis

## 5. KEY INSTITUTIONS AND MILESTONES

### Major Centers of Psychology:

#### A. Calcutta University (The Birthplace)

- **First psychology department in India** (1916)
- **First experimental laboratory** (1915)
- **First Ph.D. in psychology** (Girindrasekhar Bose on "concept of repression")
- **First Applied Psychology Wing** (1938)
- Dominated by **experimental and psychoanalytic** traditions
- Silver Jubilee Session (1938): Jung, Meyers, and Spearman invited

#### B. University of Mysore

- Department established **1924**
- **M.V. Gopalswami** as head
- **Mental testing tradition** (trained with Spearman at London)
- **First animal laboratory in India**
- Focus on **intelligence testing and education**

#### C. Lucknow University

- Laboratory organized **1929**
- N.N. Sengupta moved here from Calcutta
- Co-authored "Introduction to Social Psychology" (1928) with Radhakamal Mukherjee
- **Sociological orientation** to social psychology

#### D. Patna University

- Department established **1946**
- **Institute of Psychological Research and Services**
- **H.P. Maiti** as head (trained with Bose)
- **Psychoanalytic and clinical orientation**
- Major center for **vocational guidance and counseling**

#### E. Other Institutions

- **Aligarh Muslim University**: Psychology courses
- **Panjab University**: Psychology teaching
- **Hindu University**: Psychology in philosophy

## 7. AREAS OF RESEARCH AND TEACHING

### Research Areas :

#### A. Experimental Psychology (Dominant)

- **Sensation and perception**
- **Psychophysics** (following Wundt and Titchener)
- **Reaction time** studies
- **Attention** and **motivation**
- **Depth perception**

**Characteristics**:

- Laboratory-based research
- Western models replicated
- "Value-free" natural science approach
- Little attention to cultural factors initially

#### B. Psychoanalytic Research

- **Concept of repression** (Bose's Ph.D.)
- **Unconscious processes**
- **Dream analysis**
- **Indian adaptations** of Freudian theory

#### C. Mental Testing and Intelligence

- **Intelligence test adaptation** (Gopalswami)
- **Educational applications**
- **Factor theory** (following Spearman)

#### D. Social Psychology (Emerging)

- **Social facilitation** experiments (replicating Allport)
- **Rumor study** by Prasad (1935) - during earthquake
  - Content analysis of rumors
  - Found anxiety-inducing rumors justified fear
  - **Influenced Festinger's cognitive dissonance theory**

#### E. Applied Psychology (Later Period)

- **Educational psychology**
- **Vocational guidance** (Patna)
- **Clinical psychology** (Bose's mental hospital)

### Teaching Areas :

- **General Psychology** (most widely taught)
- **Educational Psychology** (second most common)
- **Social Psychology** (growing area)
- **Experimental Psychology** (core method)
- **Abnormal Psychology**
- **Comparative Psychology**

## 9. CHARACTERISTICS OF PRE-INDEPENDENCE PSYCHOLOGY

### Key Features :

#### 1. Western Dominance

- **"Ready-made intellectual package"** from West
- Theories and methods imported from Europe and America
- Little initial adaptation to Indian context
- Replication of Western studies on Indian samples

#### 2. Imitative Nature

- Laboratory research following **Wundt and Titchener**
- Psychoanalysis following **Freud** (though creatively adapted by Bose)
- Mental testing following **Spearman**
- Social psychology following **Allport**

#### 3. Limited Institutional Base

- Only **3 universities** with psychology departments at independence
- Psychology mostly taught in **Philosophy departments**
- Limited research funding from colonial government

#### 4. Two Worlds Phenomenon

Indian psychologists lived in two different worlds:

- **Western scientific psychology** (professional identity)
- **Indian philosophical/spiritual traditions** (personal identity)

**Examples**:

- **N.N. Sengupta**: Experimental psychologist who wrote on mysticism
- **G.S. Bose**: Psychoanalyst who interpreted Gita
- **B.N. Seal**: Experimentalist and historian of Indian science

#### 5. Neglect of Social Sciences

- Colonial government discouraged social science development
- No career openings for social scientists
- Psychology did not attract "brilliant people" initially
- Focus on "safe" areas (sensation, perception) rather than social issues

#### 6. Beginning of Indigenization (Late Period)

- Some awareness of need for Indian context
- Bose's Indian psychoanalysis
- Seal's work on Indian psychology history
- Gopalswami's test adaptations

#### 7. Applied Psychology Emergence (Late 1930s-40s)

- **1938**: Applied Psychology Wing at Calcutta
- **1946**: Patna's Institute of Psychological Research and Services
- **Vocational guidance** bureaus
- **Clinical psychology** beginnings

## 10. COMPARISON WITH POST-INDEPENDENCE ERA

| Aspect                      | Pre-Independence (Before 1947)                     | Post-Independence (After 1947)               |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| **Number of Departments**   | 3 universities (Calcutta, Mysore, Patna)           | Rapid expansion to 70+ universities          |
| **Theoretical Orientation** | Western dominance; imitative                       | Questioning Western theories; indigenization |
| **Research Focus**          | Experimental, psychoanalytic, testing              | Socially relevant, problem-oriented          |
| **Methodology**             | Laboratory-based, "value-free"                     | Field studies, applied research              |
| **Cultural Context**        | Neglected; universalistic assumptions              | Emphasis on Indian context; cross-cultural   |
| **Professional Bodies**     | IPA (1916), Indian Psychoanalytical Society (1922) | Expansion; specialization (IACP, 1968)       |
| **Funding**                 | Minimal government support                         | ICSSR (1964), increased funding              |
| **Training**                | Limited; mostly abroad                             | Systematic programs in India                 |
| **Identity**                | Colonial; derivative                               | National; indigenous                         |
